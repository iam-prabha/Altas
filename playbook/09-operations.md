# 9. Operations

## The Principle

A SaaS business runs on two engines: acquisition and retention. Most founders obsess over acquisition. The best founders obsess over retention, because a leaky bucket kills any growth rate.

Operations is the system you build around your product to keep customers happy, paying, and referring. In the early days, you are the operations department. That's fine. But you need a system so you don't burn out and so customers get a consistent experience.

## The Checklist

### Onboarding (The Make-or-Break Moment)

- [ ] First login should deliver value in under 60 seconds. No empty states.
- [ ] Progress bar or checklist showing setup steps (e.g., "Connect your calendar" → "Share your link" → "Done!").
- [ ] Send a personal welcome email from the founder within 1 hour of signup.
- [ ] Offer a 15-minute setup call for all paying customers (first 50-100 customers).
- [ ] Create a 3-email onboarding sequence:
  - Day 0: Welcome + first action.
  - Day 2: Advanced tip or use case.
  - Day 7: Case study or social proof.
- [ ] Track activation rate (% of signups who complete the core action). This is your most important metric before MRR.

### Customer Support

- [ ] **First 50 customers:** You are support. Reply within 1 hour. Over-communicate.
- [ ] **50-200 customers:** Add a shared inbox (Intercom, Help Scout, or Plain).
- [ ] **200+ customers:** Knowledge base (self-serve) + email support + office hours.
- [ ] Create a "known issues" page. Update it before customers ask.
- [ ] Use Canny or a simple board for feature requests. Show customers what you're building.
- [ ] **Response time SLAs:** Emergency (down) = 1 hour. Bug = 24 hours. Feature request = 1 week with a status update.
- [ ] Every support ticket is product feedback. Track themes monthly. Ship fixes for the top 3.

### Retention & Churn Reduction

- [ ] Track daily active users (DAU) or weekly active users (WAU). Is usage going up or down?
- [ ] Set up churn triggers: if a user hasn't logged in for 7/14/30 days, send a re-engagement email.
- [ ] Survey churned customers: "What would have kept you?" Record every answer.
- [ ] Common churn reasons and fixes:
  - Didn't see value → improve onboarding.
  - Too expensive → offer a cheaper plan or usage-based pricing.
  - Missing feature → prioritize it or explain the roadmap.
  - Using competitor → identify what they have that you don't.
- [ ] Win-back campaigns: email churned customers after 30/60/90 days with new features or offers.
- [ ] Build switching costs: integrations, saved data, team members, custom workflows.

### Metrics Dashboard

Track these weekly from day 1:

| Metric | Why |
|--------|-----|
| MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue) | The north star. |
| New customers | Leading indicator of growth. |
| Churn rate (logo + revenue) | Are you leaking? |
| LTV (Lifetime Value) | How much each customer is worth. |
| CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) | How much you spend to get one. |
| LTV:CAC ratio | Is the business model sustainable? |
| Activation rate | Are signups becoming users? |
| DAU / WAU | Are users coming back? |
| Support tickets | Is the product stable? |
| Net Promoter Score (NPS) | Would customers recommend you? |

> Use a simple spreadsheet or a tool like Baremetrics/ChartMogul for automated MRR tracking.

### Billing & Operations

- [ ] Set up dunning (automatic retry on failed payments). Stripe does this. Enable it.
- [ ] Send invoices and receipts automatically.
- [ ] Have a cancellation flow: ask why, offer alternatives (pause subscription, downgrade plan, 30-day free extension).
- [ ] Track upgrades and downgrades. Upgrades mean you're delivering value. Downgrades mean price sensitivity or feature mismatch.
- [ ] Set a monthly "operations review" calendar reminder: review metrics, support themes, churn reasons.

## Case Study: Intercom

Intercom pioneered the "proactive support" model — instead of waiting for customers to email, they'd message users in-app based on behavior (e.g., "You've been on this page for 30 seconds. Need help?").

**Why this worked for them (and for your customers):**
- Reduced support volume by solving problems before they became tickets.
- Increased trial-to-paid conversion by helping users get unstuck.
- Built a relationship with users at scale.

**Key takeaway:** Operations isn't just reactive. Proactive interventions — triggered emails, in-app messages, usage alerts — reduce churn and support load.

## Case Study: ConvertKit

ConvertKit's onboarding was famously manual. Nathan Barry personally onboarded every new customer with a 15-30 minute screen-sharing call. For a SaaS selling to 500+ customers.

**Why this worked:**
- Zero churn from customers he onboarded personally.
- Deep understanding of customer pain (fed product roadmap).
- Customers felt invested in his success (reciprocity).
- Word-of-mouth exploded because those customers became evangelists.

**When he stopped:** When onboarding became a bottleneck (too many customers), ConvertKit replaced it with video courses, templates, and a knowledge base — all informed by the hundreds of onboarding calls he had done.

**Key takeaway:** Do things that don't scale. They build the product and customer knowledge you can't get any other way.

## Red Flags

| Mistake | Why It's Dangerous |
|---------|-------------------|
| No onboarding sequence | Most signups will never activate without guidance. |
| Ignoring support for "building features" | Support IS product development. Every ticket tells you what's broken. |
| Measuring vanity metrics | "Total registered users" means nothing. Active paying users means everything. |
| No churn analysis | You can't fix churn if you don't know why it happens. |
| Manual processes for everything | Automate billing, email, and reporting early. Your time is for customers and product. |
| Not talking to churned customers | They're your best source of product truth. Bribe them with a $20 gift card for a 15-minute call. |
