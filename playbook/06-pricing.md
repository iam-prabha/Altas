# 6. Pricing

## The Principle

Pricing is the most underrated product decision. It determines your positioning, your customer expectations, your growth rate, and your profitability. Price too low and you'll never build a sustainable business. Price too high and you'll struggle to get traction.

The right price is the highest number your target customer will pay without hesitation, given the value they perceive. You won't get it right on the first try. Plan to change it.

## The Checklist

### Choose a Pricing Model

- [ ] **Flat-rate:** One price, one plan. Simple. Works for single-feature tools (e.g., Calendly starter).
- [ ] **Tiered (Good-Better-Best):** Most common. 3 plans. Base covers costs. Mid is for most customers. Top captures power users.
- [ ] **Usage-based:** Pay per API call, per seat, per document, per customer. Scales with value. Common for infrastructure.
- [ ] **Per-seat:** Charge per user. Good for collaboration tools. Can limit growth (users cost money).
- [ ] **Hybrid:** Base fee + usage overage. Best of both worlds (e.g., Slack: per-seat base + no overage for small teams).
- [ ] **One-time + subscription:** One-time setup fee + monthly maintenance. Common in enterprise.

### Set Price Anchors

- [ ] Research what competitors charge. Your price should be in the same ballpark unless you have a clear differentiator.
- [ ] Low-end anchor: shows you're accessible. High-end anchor: makes the mid-tier look reasonable.
- [ ] Never put your most popular plan as the cheapest. People like the middle option.
- [ ] Dollar amounts ending in 9 or 7 convert better than round numbers ($29 vs $30).

### The Pricing Psychology Playbook

- [ ] **Decoy effect:** Offer a deliberately unappealing expensive plan to make the mid-tier look good.
- [ ] **Relative pricing:** A $50 plan looks expensive next to a $10 plan. It looks cheap next to a $200 plan.
- [ ] **Annual discount:** Offer 2-3 months free for annual billing. Improves cash flow, reduces churn.
- [ ] **Free trial, not freemium:** Time-limited trials convert better than feature-limited free plans (for most B2B). Exceptions: products with strong network effects.
- [ ] **Money-back guarantee:** Reduces purchase anxiety. Use for higher-priced plans.

### Price for Your Stage

- [ ] **Pre-revenue / validation:** Set a price. Test it. If nobody buys, lower it. If everyone buys without hesitation, raise it.
- [ ] **Early stage ($0-10k MRR):** Optimize for conversion, not margin. Get customers in the door.
- [ ] **Growth stage ($10-100k MRR):** Start optimizing for LTV. Raise prices for new customers. Introduce tiers.
- [ ] **Scale stage ($100k+ MRR):** Enterprise pricing (custom quotes, contracts, implementation fees).

### When to Raise Prices

- [ ] **When you add significant value:** New features, faster performance, better support.
- [ ] **When churn is low (<5% monthly):** Your customers are sticky. They can handle a price increase.
- [ ] **When you're growing fast:** High demand means your price may be too low.
- [ ] **Grandfather existing customers:** Don't raise prices on existing customers. Raise them for new ones.
- [ ] **Communicate the increase:** Share what's improved, give 30 days notice.

### Common Pricing Mistakes

- [ ] Pricing based on cost (cost-plus). Price based on value, not your costs.
- [ ] Pricing too low to get traction. You attract the wrong customers (price-sensitive, high churn).
- [ ] Pricing too high without justification. You need social proof, feature parity, or brand trust.
- [ ] Changing pricing too often. Destroys trust. Change no more than 2x per year.
- [ ] Not testing. Run A/B tests on pricing pages. Try different tiers, different price points.

## Case Study: Basecamp

Basecamp famously charges a flat $99/month for unlimited users. No per-seat pricing.

**Why this worked:**
- **Simplified decision-making:** Customers don't have to calculate cost per user.
- **Encourages adoption:** No friction to add more team members.
- **Trust builder:** "We're not going to nickel-and-dime you."
- **Differentiator:** Every competitor charged per-seat. Basecamp stood out.

**The trade-off:** They cap revenue per customer at $99/month. To grow, they need more customers, not more revenue per customer. This works for them because their market is huge and their churn is low.

**Key takeaway:** Your pricing model is a strategic decision, not an accounting one. It communicates your values and positions you against competitors.

## Case Study: Calendly (Freemium → Paid progression)

Calendly started free. As they added value, they introduced paid tiers:
- **Free:** 1 event type, basic calendar integrations.
- **Premium ($8/month):** Multiple event types, custom branding, reminders.
- **Pro ($12/month):** Team scheduling, workflows, CRM integrations.

**Why this worked:**
- Free plan is the funnel top. Millions of users discover Calendly for free.
- As users get more serious (or use it for business), they need Pro features.
- Pricing is low enough to be an impulse buy.

**Key takeaway:** Freemium works when your free users are also your marketing channel (every meeting = Calendly branding). Paid plans unlock the features businesses need.

## Red Flags

| Mistake | Why It's Dangerous |
|---------|-------------------|
| Pricing too low | You attract price-sensitive customers who churn fast. You can't afford support. |
| Pricing too high without value signal | Visitors bounce. You need social proof, case studies, and trust before high prices work. |
| Too many plans | Analysis paralysis. 3 plans is the sweet spot. |
| Hiding pricing | "Contact us for pricing" works only for enterprise ($500+/month). For everyone else, show prices. |
| Not changing prices | Your first pricing guess is wrong. Iterate. |
| Charging less than $10/month | Below $10, customer support costs eat your margin. $20-50/month is the indie SaaS sweet spot. |
