# 3. Market Research

## The Principle

Market research is not a corporate exercise. It's how you know whether your idea has a chance before you invest months of your life.

The goal is simple: estimate how many people have this problem, how much they currently spend solving it (or how much the pain is worth), and whether the market is growing. You don't need perfect numbers. You need directional answers that keep you from building something nobody wants.

## The Checklist

### Size the Market (TAM / SAM / SOM)

- [ ] **TAM (Total Addressable Market):** The total revenue opportunity if 100% of the market used your product. Broad estimate. Example: "Global project management software market = $8B."
- [ ] **SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market):** The portion you can actually reach with your distribution model. Example: "Project management for remote-first teams of 5-50 people."
- [ ] **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):** The portion you can realistically capture in 3-5 years. Be honest. Example: "2% of the remote team project management space = $X."
- [ ] Use public sources: Gartner, Forrester, IBISWorld, Statista, industry reports.
- [ ] Use bottom-up: Count potential customers in your target segment × average price point.

### Map the Competition

- [ ] List direct competitors (same problem, same solution approach).
- [ ] List indirect competitors (same problem, different solution approach — e.g., spreadsheets).
- [ ] List future competitors (adjacent companies who could easily enter your space).
- [ ] For each competitor, note: pricing, target customer, strengths, weaknesses.
- [ ] Read their 2-star and 3-star reviews religiously. Every complaint is a feature opportunity.
- [ ] Sign up for their product. Go through their onboarding. Feel the pain.

### Identify the Incumbent's Blind Spots

- [ ] What do competitors ignore because it's "too small" a segment?
- [ ] What's broken about their onboarding?
- [ ] Where is their customer support slow or rude?
- [ ] What features do they charge extra for that could be included?
- [ ] What do power users hack around instead of using built-in features?

### Customer Interviews (The Highest-ROI Activity)

- [ ] Interview 10-20 people who match your target customer profile.
- [ ] **DO NOT pitch your idea.** Ask about their current workflow, frustrations, and workarounds.
- [ ] Use the template in `templates/customer-interview-guide.md`.
- [ ] Record and transcribe every interview. Look for patterns.
- [ ] If 3+ people independently describe the same pain, you have something.

### Validate Willingness to Pay

- [ ] Ask: "If there were a tool that solved [specific pain], how much would you pay per month?"
- [ ] Ask: "Are you currently paying for anything to solve this?" — if yes, you have validation.
- [ ] Ask: "What would make this a must-buy vs. a nice-to-have?"
- [ ] Don't trust enthusiastic verbal answers. Actions matter more.

### Analyze Market Trends

- [ ] Is the market growing, shrinking, or flat? (Growing markets forgive mistakes.)
- [ ] Are there new regulations, technologies, or behaviors creating tailwinds?
- [ ] Is the customer base getting more or less technical?
- [ ] Are there adjacent markets you can expand into later?

## Case Study: Ahrefs

Ahrefs entered the SEO tools market when Moz and Majestic were dominant. They didn't try to be a "better Moz." They focused on one thing Moz was bad at: backlink data freshness.

**The research:**
- SEO professionals complained constantly that Moz's index was weeks old.
- The pain was acute — stale data means bad SEO decisions.
- The market was growing (content marketing explosion).
- Existing solutions were expensive and slow.

**The strategy:**
- Build the freshest, most complete backlink index.
- Target power users who felt Moz wasn't keeping up.
- Charge competitive prices with better data.
- Let the data quality speak for itself.

**Key takeaway:** You don't need to beat the market leader on everything. Find the one thing they're bad at and be 10x better at it. That's your wedge.

## Red Flags

| Mistake | Why It's Dangerous |
|---------|-------------------|
| Believing your own estimates | TAM slides are fiction. Validate with real conversations. |
| Ignoring competitors | "We have no competitors" means either (a) there's no market or (b) you haven't looked hard enough. |
| Talking to friends and family | They'll be nice. You'll be misled. Talk to strangers who fit your ICP. |
| Asking leading questions | "Would you use this?" — everyone says yes. "Walk me through how you solve X today" — honest answer. |
| Skipping this step | You can build a great product for a market that doesn't exist. That's called a hobby. |
