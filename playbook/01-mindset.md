# 1. The SaaS Mindset

## The Principle

Every SaaS business is a math problem. Revenue minus costs over time. If you understand the math, you stop gambling and start engineering outcomes.

Most first-time founders obsess over the product. Successful ones obsess over the unit economics — how much it costs to acquire a customer (CAC), how much they pay over time (LTV), and how many leave (churn). Get these three numbers right and you can build a $10M+ business on a laptop.

## The Checklist

### Understand the Core Metrics

- [ ] **ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue)** = MRR × 12. The north star.
- [ ] **MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue)** = paying customers × average revenue per account.
- [ ] **LTV (Lifetime Value)** = average revenue per customer over their entire relationship with you.
- [ ] **CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost)** = total sales & marketing spend / new customers acquired.
- [ ] **Churn Rate** = customers lost in a period / customers at the start.
- [ ] **LTV:CAC Ratio** — aim for at least 3:1. Below that, you're losing money on every customer.
- [ ] **Payback Period** = CAC / (monthly revenue per customer). Should be under 12 months.

### Adopt the Boring SaaS Thesis

- [ ] Target boring, unsexy industries (insurance, logistics, construction, compliance, HR).
- [ ] Incumbents are slow, expensive, and hated by their users.
- [ ] You don't need a novel idea. You need a better execution of an existing need.
- [ ] Look for industries where software is 10+ years old and nobody has innovated.

### Pick the Right Business Model

- [ ] **Subscription (SaaS):** Predictable revenue, higher LTV, requires ongoing value delivery.
- [ ] **Usage-based:** Scales with customer success, good for APIs and infrastructure.
- [ ] **Freemium → Paid:** High top-of-funnel, requires strong conversion mechanics.
- [ ] **Marketplace:** Take rate model, chicken-and-egg problem to solve.

### Set Your North Star

- [ ] Decide: are you building a lifestyle business ($1-5M ARR), a ramen-profitable indie co ($5-20M ARR), or a venture-scale company ($50M+ ARR)?
- [ ] Your goal determines your strategy. Don't copy VC-backed tactics if you're bootstrapping.
- [ ] Write down your target ARR for year 1, year 3, year 5.

## Case Study: Plausible

Plausible is a simple web analytics tool. They compete with Google Analytics (free). Sounds impossible, right?

**The mindset shift:** They didn't compete on features. They competed on privacy, simplicity, and owning your data. They targeted a specific pain (Google Analytics is slow, creepy, and complex) for a specific audience (privacy-conscious indie makers and small businesses).

**The math:**
- $9/month per site (cheap enough to impulse-buy, expensive enough to be a real business)
- Low churn (analytics is sticky — once you set it up, you don't leave)
- Tiny team (3 founders for a long time, grew to ~10 at $2M+ ARR)
- No VC money — bootstrapped and profitable from year one

**Key takeaway:** You don't need to beat the giant. You need to be the best option for a specific group that the giant ignores.

## Red Flags

| Mistake | Why It Hurts |
|---------|-------------|
| Building for "everyone" | You can't market to everyone. You can't support everyone. You fail everyone. |
| Obsessing over features before pricing | If you don't know how to charge, you don't have a business — you have a hobby. |
| Pivoting every month | Give a strategy 6 months to work. Changing course weekly means you never leave the harbor. |
| Ignoring churn | Growing fast but leaking customers is like filling a bucket with a hole in it. Fix retention before acquisition. |
| Copying VC SaaS pricing | VC-backed companies can charge less (or nothing) because they're funded. You can't. |
