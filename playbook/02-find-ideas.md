# 2. Find Ideas

## The Principle

The best SaaS ideas are already hiding in plain sight. They live inside complaints, manual workflows, spreadsheets being used as databases, and industries where everyone says "that's just how it's done."

You don't need a flashy idea. You need a real problem that someone will pay to have solved. The difference between a hobby and a business is *willingness to pay*.

## The Checklist

### Mine Your Own Experience

- [ ] List every job you've ever had. What software did you hate using?
- [ ] What repetitive task did you do manually that felt like it should be automated?
- [ ] What spreadsheets did you maintain that should have been a database?
- [ ] What did your boss complain about weekly?
- [ ] What process in your industry hasn't changed in 10+ years?

### Scrape Complaints (The Gold Mine)

- [ ] Search Reddit for "I wish there was a tool that..." in relevant subreddits.
- [ ] Read G2/Capterra reviews for existing SaaS products — sort by 2-star and 3-star reviews (those are features people will pay for).
- [ ] Browse "Show HN" and "Product Hunt" comments — what do people ask for that doesn't exist?
- [ ] Look at "Request a Feature" pages for existing products in your target market.
- [ ] Monitor Quora, Hacker News "Ask HN", and Indie Hackers for recurring pain points.

### Analyze Existing Products

- [ ] Pick a popular SaaS in a boring niche. What features do 2-star reviewers complain are missing?
- [ ] What would it look like if you built a simpler, cheaper version?
- [ ] What would it look like if you built a premium, focused version for power users?
- [ ] What would it look like if you removed all the enterprise bloat and sold to small businesses?

### Look at Industry Shifts

- [ ] New regulations create new software needs (GDPR, HIPAA, SOC2, etc.).
- [ ] Remote work created entire categories (async video, digital whiteboards, virtual events).
- [ ] AI API availability enables old ideas that were previously impossible.
- [ ] What recently became cheap enough (APIs, cloud infra) to make a new SaaS viable?

### The "Micro-SaaS" Hunting Grounds

- [ ] Stripe / Shopify / Salesforce app marketplaces — build on top of existing platforms.
- [ ] Slack / Teams / Discord bots — solve a specific chat-based workflow.
- [ ] Chrome extensions that connect to a paid backend.
- [ ] API wrappers that simplify a complex API into one useful endpoint.

### The Idea Filter (Must Answer All)

- [ ] Does this save people money or time?
- [ ] Is someone already paying for a worse solution to this problem?
- [ ] Can I charge $10+/month for this?
- [ ] Is this a pain that repeats (monthly, weekly, daily)?
- [ ] Can I reach the first 100 customers without spending on ads?
- [ ] Can a single developer build an MVP of this in 4-8 weeks?

> **If you can't answer YES to all six, keep looking.**

## Case Study: Calendly

Calendly started as a founder's personal pain. Tope Awotona was a sales guy who hated the back-and-forth of scheduling meetings. He looked for a tool that solved it. Nothing good existed.

**The idea:**
- A scheduling link that shows your availability and lets people book without emailing.
- Simple enough to explain in one sentence.
- Painful enough that people would pay to make it go away.

**Why it worked:**
- The pain was universal (every knowledge worker schedules meetings).
- The existing solution (email ping-pong) was universally hated.
- It's sticky — once you set up your Calendly link, switching costs are high.
- Virality built in — every meeting you schedule with someone outside your company is a product demo.

**Key takeaway:** The best ideas come from your own life. You are the customer. You feel the pain. You know exactly what a fix looks like.

## Red Flags

| Mistake | Why It's Dangerous |
|---------|-------------------|
| "I'll build it and they'll come" | Building is the easy part. Distribution is the hard part. Validate demand before writing code. |
| The idea is a feature, not a product | If your idea could be a checkbox in an existing product, it's not a business. |
| No one has tried this before | Usually means there's no demand, not that you're a genius pioneer. |
| You can't explain it in one sentence | If you can't, you don't understand the problem well enough. |
| Target market is "everyone" | See chapter 1. "Everyone" means no one. |
