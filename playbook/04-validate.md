# 4. Validate Before You Build

## The Principle

The most expensive way to validate an idea is to build it first. The cheapest way is to set up a landing page, drive some targeted traffic, and see if people click "Buy" or enter their email.

Your goal in validation is **not** to prove your idea is great. It's to prove it's worth building. Be ruthless. If the smoke test fails, that's a success — you just saved yourself 3 months of work.

## The Checklist

### Build a Smoke-Test Landing Page

- [ ] Single page. Headline describes the problem, not your solution.
- [ ] Subheadline describes the outcome the customer wants.
- [ ] 3-5 bullet points of benefits (not features).
- [ ] A "Buy Now" or "Pre-order" button with a real price.
- [ ] No "Coming Soon" language. Make it feel like a real product.

> See `templates/smoke-test-page-outline.md` for the exact structure.

### Drive Targeted Traffic

- [ ] Post in niche communities where your target customers hang out (Reddit, Slack groups, Facebook groups, niche forums).
- [ ] Write a post about the *problem* (not your product). Ask the community how they solve it.
- [ ] Share your smoke test in a "I built a landing page for a problem I see — would this help you?" way.
- [ ] Run $50-100 in targeted ads if you have a clear audience and the LTV math works.
- [ ] Cold email 20-50 potential customers with a genuine ask for feedback, link to the page.

### Measure Real Signals

- [ ] **Email signups:** Someone who enters their email is mildly interested.
- [ ] **Credit card charges:** Someone who pays is committed. This is the gold standard.
- [ ] **Click-through rate from post to page:** >5% is strong interest.
- [ ] **Conversion rate from visitor to signup:** >3-5% for free interest, >1-2% for paid pre-orders.
- [ ] **Bounce rate:** High bounce means your message doesn't match the audience or your page isn't convincing.

### Run the "Willing to Pay" Test

- [ ] Offer a pre-sale / founding member discount (e.g., "50% off for life if you buy before we launch").
- [ ] If people won't pre-order at a discount, they definitely won't pay full price later.
- [ ] If you get 5+ pre-orders from cold traffic, you have real validation.
- [ ] If you get 0 pre-orders, either (a) you haven't reached the right people, (b) the price is wrong, or (c) the problem isn't painful enough.

### Alternatives to Smoke Tests

- [ ] **Concierge MVP:** Do the work manually for a few customers. Prove the value before automating.
- [ ] **Demo video:** Dropbox-style. Show the product working (even if it's fake screenshots).
- [ ] **Waitlist landing page:** Weaker signal than pre-orders, but useful for marketplaces (need supply + demand).
- [ ] **Crowdfunding (Kickstarter/Indiegogo):** Works for B2C tools with a story.
- [ ] **Content-first validation:** Start the blog/newsletter for your target audience before building. If you can't get readers, you won't get customers.

### The Validation Gate

- [ ] **Gate condition:** Before writing a single line of code, you must have evidence that real strangers will pay for your solution.
- [ ] If you don't have this evidence, don't build. Do more research. Adjust the problem statement. Find a different audience.

## Case Study: Plausible (Validation)

Plausible's founder, Uku Taht, didn't start by building analytics software. He wrote a blog post titled "Google Analytics is bloated. Here's an alternative."

- The post went viral on Hacker News.
- Hundreds of people signed up for a mailing list.
- He set up a landing page with a pre-order button at $9/month.
- 50+ people paid before he wrote any code.

He used that $450/month in pre-orders to fund the initial development and validate that people would pay.

**Key takeaway:** Plausible validated demand and secured revenue *before* writing code. When they finally launched, they already had customers waiting.

## Red Flags

| Mistake | Why It's Dangerous |
|---------|-------------------|
| Building a full product before validation | Wastes 3-6 months on something nobody wants. |
| Ignoring weak signals | "10 people signed up" is not validation if you emailed 10,000 people. |
| Mistaking friends/family interest for market demand | They lie to be nice. Strangers with credit cards tell the truth. |
| Calling it validated because of compliments | Compliments are free. Pre-orders are real. |
| Building based on one enthusiastic person | One person is an anecdote. Five paying strangers is a pattern. |
| Skipping the price test | If you don't know the price, you don't know if the economics work. |
