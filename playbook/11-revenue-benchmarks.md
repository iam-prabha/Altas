# 11. Revenue Benchmarks

## The Principle

Revenue is a lagging indicator. By the time you see it in your bank account, the decisions that created it were made weeks or months ago. Understanding the benchmarks at each stage helps you know where you are, what to focus on, and whether you're on track.

The numbers below are directional. Actual SaaS benchmarks vary by vertical, pricing, and geography. But the *patterns* are universal.

## The Checklist

### Stage 0: Pre-Revenue (Validation)

| Metric | Benchmark |
|--------|-----------|
| Time in this stage | 1-6 months |
| Team size | Solo or 2 co-founders |
| Spend | $0-2k/month (your living costs + hosting + tools) |
| Key activity | Customer interviews, smoke tests, pre-sells |

**Goal:** Get your first $1k MRR from people you don't know personally.
**If stuck:** Keep talking to customers. Your problem statement isn't painful enough.

### Stage 1: $0 → $1k MRR (Traction)

| Metric | Benchmark |
|--------|-----------|
| Target | $1k MRR |
| Time to hit | 3-9 months |
| Team | Solo or 2 people |
| Customers | 20-50 (at $20-50/month) |
| Monthly spend | $500-3k |
| Churn | <10% monthly (preferably <5%) |
| CAC | $0-200 (mostly founder effort, no paid ads) |

**Goal:** Find a repeatable acquisition channel. One channel that works is enough.
**Reality check:** If you can't get to $1k MRR in 9 months, your idea, market, or execution needs rethinking.

### Stage 2: $1k → $10k MRR (Product-Market Fit)

| Metric | Benchmark |
|--------|-----------|
| Target | $10k MRR |
| Time to hit | 6-18 months from $1k |
| Team | 2-5 people |
| Customers | 100-500 |
| Monthly spend | $3-15k |
| Churn | <5% monthly (or <40% annual) |
| CAC | $50-500 (may start light paid acquisition) |
| LTV:CAC | >3:1 |

**Goal:** Prove the unit economics work. Your churn is low, your acquisition channel is predictable, and customers stay.
**Best practice:** Hire your first support person. Automated onboarding. Start content marketing in earnest.

**Revenue distribution ($10k MRR):**
- Bottom 25% of SaaS: $2-5k MRR
- Median: $10k MRR
- Top 25%: $15-20k MRR
- Source: Baremetrics, OpenView benchmarks

### Stage 3: $10k → $100k MRR (Scale)

| Metric | Benchmark |
|--------|-----------|
| Target | $100k MRR |
| Time to hit | 12-36 months from $10k |
| Team | 5-20 people |
| Customers | 500-2,000+ |
| Monthly spend | $15-80k |
| Churn | <3% monthly (or <20% annual) |
| CAC | $200-2,000 (may include sales team) |
| LTV:CAC | >5:1 |

**Goal:** Systematize growth. Multiple acquisition channels. Team in place. Product maturing.
**Best practice:** Hire sales (if B2B). Add enterprise pricing. Internationalization. Kill features that don't serve the core.

**Revenue distribution ($100k MRR = $1.2M ARR):**
- If bootstrapped: You're profitable and paying yourself well.
- If VC-backed: You're raising Series A or growing toward it.
- This is the "default alive" zone — the business can survive without additional funding.

### Stage 4: $100k MRR+ (Market Leader)

| Metric | Benchmark |
|--------|-----------|
| ARR | $1.2M+ |
| Team | 20-100+ |
| Customers | 2,000+ |
| Churn | <2% monthly (or <15% annual) |
| Revenue growth | 20-100% year-over-year |

**Goal:** Defend market position. Expand to adjacent segments. Become the category default.
**Best practice:** This is where you have an executive team, board meetings, and play the game differently. Most indie founders stop here or sell the business.

### Revenue Multiples (If Selling)

| Stage | Multiple of ARR |
|-------|-----------------|
| <$1M ARR | 3-5x (usually acqui-hire or talent deal) |
| $1-5M ARR | 4-6x |
| $5-20M ARR | 5-8x |
| $20M+ ARR | 8-15x+ (strategic buyers pay premiums) |

These vary wildly by growth rate, profitability, and market. A 100% growing SaaS trades at 2x the multiple of a 20% growing one.

### Key Benchmarks Summary

| Metric | Healthy SaaS | Danger Zone |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| Monthly churn (logo) | <5% | >10% |
| Monthly churn (revenue) | <2% | >5% |
| LTV:CAC | >3:1 | <1:1 |
| Payback period | <12 months | >18 months |
| Gross margin | >70% | <60% |
| Activation rate | >40% | <20% |
| DAU/MAU ratio | >20% | <10% |
| NPS | >40 | <20 |

## Case Study: Baremetrics (The Open Benchmarks)

Baremetrics publishes real SaaS benchmarks from their customer base. As of their most recent data:
- Median SaaS company does ~$5k MRR after 2 years.
- Top quartile does ~$15k MRR.
- Average churn across all SaaS: 4-6% monthly.
- Best-in-class churn: 1-2% monthly (for B2B SaaS with high switching costs).

The median SaaS company is a sustainable lifestyle business. The top quartile are the ones that break out. Both are valid outcomes.

**Key takeaway:** Don't compare yourself to unicorns. Compare yourself to the median SaaS. If you're above median, you're doing well. The goal is to get to the top quartile of your peer group.

## Case Study: OpenView's SaaS Benchmarks

OpenView's annual SaaS benchmarks show a clear pattern:
- Companies with lowest churn grow fastest — even with slower new customer acquisition.
- Companies that raise prices annually grow MRR 2x faster than those that don't.
- Companies with self-serve onboarding grow faster than those requiring demos (at the low end).
- Companies that track metrics weekly outperform those that track monthly.

**Key takeaway:** The decisions that seem small (tracking metrics weekly, raising prices, reducing churn) compound dramatically over 24-36 months.

## Red Flags

| Mistake | Why It's Dangerous |
|---------|-------------------|
| Comparing to VC-backed unicorns | They play a different game (growth at all costs). You might need profit from day 1. |
| Ignoring benchmarks entirely | You won't know if you're doing well or drowning until it's too late. |
| Fixating on $10k MRR | It's a milestone, not a destination. Keep building. |
| Not celebrating milestones | $1k MRR is harder than $100k MRR. Celebrate. It's rare. |
| Raising prices only when in trouble | Regular price increases signal confidence. Reactive increases signal desperation. |
| Thinking all revenue is good revenue | High-churn revenue destroys your business. Low-churn revenue builds it. |
