# 5. Customer Archetypes

## The Principle

You cannot build for "everyone." If you try, you build for no one. The most successful SaaS companies serve a very specific type of customer extraordinarily well and let everyone else go.

Your Ideal Customer Profile (ICP) is the single person whose problem you solve, whose budget you fit, and whose workflow you improve. Everything — marketing, product, pricing, support — flows from this definition.

## The Checklist

### Define Your ICP

- [ ] **Industry:** What vertical do they work in?
- [ ] **Company size:** Solo / 2-10 / 10-50 / 50-200 / 200+?
- [ ] **Role / Title:** Who actually uses the product? Who decides to buy?
- [ ] **Technical level:** Can they self-serve or do they need hand-holding?
- [ ] **Budget:** What's their monthly software budget? Do they have a budget at all?
- [ ] **Primary pain:** What's the #1 problem your product solves for them?
- [ ] **Alternative:** What do they use today (even if it's a spreadsheet)?

### Understand the Buying Dynamics

- [ ] **User vs. Buyer:** These are often different people. The user feels the pain. The buyer controls the budget.
- [ ] **Champion:** The person inside the company who wants your product to succeed. Find them.
- [ ] **Decision-maker:** The person who signs the check. What do they care about? ROI, security, compliance, ease of use?
- [ ] **Influencer:** The person the decision-maker trusts. IT, legal, the CEO's assistant.
- [ ] **Saboteur:** Who loses if your product wins? Internal IT (if you bypass them), the incumbent vendor, the person who implemented the current solution.

**For B2C:**
- [ ] The user and buyer are the same person.
- [ ] Decisions are emotional + rational. Reduce friction.
- [ ] Price sensitivity is higher. Trial-to-paid conversion is critical.

**For B2B:**
- [ ] User and buyer are usually different.
- [ ] Decisions are rational + political. Build for the user, sell to the buyer.
- [ ] Multiple stakeholders need convincing. Sales cycles are longer.

**For Marketplaces:**
- [ ] You have two (or more) distinct customer types. Each needs a different ICP.
- [ ] Which side is harder to acquire? That's your "chicken." Solve for them first.
- [ ] Liquidity threshold: how many suppliers and consumers do you need before the marketplace works?

### Segment Your Market

- [ ] **Horizontal:** Sell to the same role across many industries (e.g., "project managers everywhere").
- [ ] **Vertical:** Sell to many roles within one industry (e.g., "everyone in dental clinics").
- [ ] **Beachhead:** The smallest possible segment you can dominate before expanding.
- [ ] Think: "Who needs this so badly they'd use a half-baked version 1?"

### Build Early Adopter Personas

- [ ] **The Innovator:** Will try anything new. Forgiving of bugs. High feedback value. Not representative of the mass market.
- [ ] **The Early Adopter:** Has the pain acutely. Actively searching for solutions. Willing to pay for a v1.
- [ ] **The Early Majority:** Needs a polished product, case studies, social proof. This is where scale begins.
- [ ] Your first 10 customers should be innovators or early adopters. Don't expect early majority until you have 100+ customers.

### Create Rejection Criteria

- [ ] Define who you *won't* sell to. This is as important as defining who you will.
- [ ] If a prospect doesn't match your ICP, pass on them. They'll churn fast and cost you support time.
- [ ] Examples: "We don't sell to enterprises over 500 people." Or "We don't serve B2C customers."

## Case Study: HubSpot

HubSpot famously defined their ICP as "SMEs in the US with 2-25 employees, selling B2B, with a website but no systematic marketing process."

**Why this worked:**
- This segment was ignored by every CRM/marketing vendor (they all targeted enterprise).
- The pain was real (small businesses had zero marketing infrastructure).
- The persona was consistent (owner-operators, hands-on, budget-conscious).
- As HubSpot grew, they expanded upmarket (SME → mid-market → enterprise).

**Key takeaway:** Start with a tight ICP that incumbents ignore. Dominate that segment. Expand later.

## Case Study: Slack

Slack's initial ICP wasn't "every company." It was **tech teams at startups that already used IRC or HipChat.**

- They understood the user (engineers, chat-native).
- They understood the buyer (CTO / VP Eng, tired of internal tooling complaints).
- They didn't pitch to non-tech companies until years later.

**Key takeaway:** Your first ICP is often the one closest to you — people like you who share the same pain.

## Red Flags

| Mistake | Why It's Dangerous |
|---------|-------------------|
| ICP is too broad | You can't write ads, content, or product copy that resonates with everyone. |
| ICP is too narrow | If only 500 companies match your ICP, you have a consulting business, not a SaaS. |
| Confusing user and buyer | Building a product users love but no one buys. |
| Selling to anyone who says yes | You'll attract the wrong customers, burn support, and inflate churn. |
| Designing for yourself | Your early adopter is not you. Talk to them. Watch them work. |
